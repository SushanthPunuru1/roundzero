Every lesson in this domain so far has covered one *piece* of a machine —
the OS itself, accounts and permissions, services, ports, passwords. This
lesson ties them together and names the thing you actually do to all of
them: **hardening**.

## Hardening is reducing what could go wrong

"Harden a machine" means systematically reducing its weaknesses — closing
unnecessary doors, tightening loose permissions, removing accounts that
shouldn't exist, enforcing a real password policy — rather than adding one
single security product and calling it done. Every earlier lesson in this
domain named a *category* of weakness (an over-privileged account, an
unneeded service, an open port, a weak password). Hardening is the verb:
going through those categories, one setting at a time, and closing the gap
each one represents.

There's no single "hardening switch." It's the accumulated effect of many
small, specific corrections, each one small enough to check and verify on
its own.

## The loop: find, fix, verify

Every individual piece of hardening work follows the same three-step
loop, and skipping the third step is the single most common mistake new
competitors make:

1. **Find** — identify a specific weakness. An account that shouldn't
   exist. A service that shouldn't be running. A password policy that's
   too weak.
2. **Fix** — make the specific change that corrects it. Delete the
   account. Stop and disable the service. Set the policy value.
3. **Verify** — confirm the fix actually took effect. Log back in and
   check the account is really gone. Confirm the service is really
   stopped. Re-read the policy setting back out and confirm it changed.

Verify is easy to skip under time pressure, because most fixes *look*
like they worked the moment you make them. But changes fail silently more
often than you'd expect: a typo in a config file line, a service that
needs an explicit restart before a setting takes effect, a group policy
that gets overridden by something else on the machine. Without verifying,
you can walk away from a "fix" that never actually applied — and not find
out until much later, if at all.

## Why this loop, and not just "fix things fast"

It's tempting to treat hardening as a race: fix as many things as
possible, as quickly as possible. The find-fix-verify loop is slower
per-item, but it has a property that pure speed doesn't: **you always know
exactly what state the machine is actually in.** If something goes wrong
later, you have a clear, verified record of what you already
confirmed — instead of a pile of changes you *think* worked.

This is also exactly how a scoring engine (the subject of a later
lesson) evaluates your work: it doesn't care about your intentions, your
effort, or which fixes you attempted — it checks the machine's actual,
current state against a list of conditions. A fix that didn't actually
take effect earns nothing, no matter how correctly you thought you did
it. Verifying isn't extra work bolted onto hardening — it's how you find
out, before the scoring engine tells you, whether a fix is real.

## Putting the whole domain together

Look back at everything this domain has covered: what the OS is and why
it's the thing being defended, who's allowed to do what (accounts,
groups, permissions), what's quietly running (services), how it's
reachable (ports), and what protects login itself (passwords). Hardening
is the discipline of walking through all of that, methodically, applying
find-fix-verify to each weak point you locate. Every lesson from here on —
Windows-specific, Linux-specific, or Cisco-specific — is this exact same
loop, applied to a different, more specific setting each time.
